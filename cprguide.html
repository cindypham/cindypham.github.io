CPR Guide

  • Save a life with your hands free

Here's a challenge: remembering how to perform CPR after you took that class an entire year ago.

Here's a bigger challenge: making smartwatches practical.

According to the American Heart Association, 70% of Americans feel helpless to act during a cardiac emergency because they either do not know how to administer CPR or their training has significantly lapsed. CPR Guide is a smartwatch app I conceptualized and designed as a passion project with a single purpose: to guide users through the CPR process and encourage more people to take action during emergencies.




  • Research

    I conducted research to analyze the severity of cardiac arrest cases that are left untreated by bystanders and interviewed a doctor about the steps needed for an everyday man to perform CPR. I boiled down the CPR process to 3 basic steps:

    • Check for unresponsiveness. If a person is unresponsive (doesn't respond to shouts or shakes) and not breathing (or is breathing abnormally) then call 911 and begin CPR.
    • Pump chest. If the victim is still not breathing normally, coughing or moving, begin chest compressions. Push down in the center of the chest 2 inches 30 times. Pump hard and fast at the rate of at least 100/minute, faster than once per second.
    • Blow. Tilt the head back and lift the chin. Pinch nose and cover the mouth with yours and blow until you see the chest rise. Give 2 breaths. Each breath should take 1 second.



  • User Personas

    I sought out people with a range of experience with health care emergencies as well as using smartwatch apps. Using a combination of survey responses and in-depth interviews, I asked people to walk me through their reactions during an emergency and elaborate on what they found difficult or scary. These were the common pain points that I derived:

    • "I don't know how many times to compress." Many people don't remember the exact pace for performing compressions other than the rate for infants is faster than for adults.
    • "How do I know for sure their heart stopped?" People are reluctant about committing to CPR if the victim might not need it. Several newbies did not trust their ability to detect a pulse manually.
    • "I just feel like this should be left to the experts." The classic bystander effect: believing someone else will step in to help because the user does not believe they are capable on their own.




  • Solutions

    Based on my research and user interviews, I conceptualized and designed CPR Guide with these key features:

    • Heart monitor. The user checks for a pulse by pressing the watch to the patient's wrist. The results display a BPM that determines whether the patient needs CPR or not.
    • Patient specifics. The user selects the age range of the patient. The watch does the hard work of calculating the appropriate compression rate.
    • Vibration for each compression. The watch vibrates with each compression that the user has to make.

Afterthoughts

CPR Guide is a passion project that is constantly being revised and improved upon.

Successes

  • Hierarchical navigation. Drilled down a series of options in an easy-to-read sequence of events.
  • Glanceability. Provided timely, glanceable information by only showing what's essential for the user to complete the task.
  • Context. Took advantage of a wearable's sensors and gather data for pulse and compression rate, saving the user time and stress.

Improvements

  • How can this process be faster? Time is essential during emergencies. If the user decides to use the smartwatch to check the victim's pulse, they will have to take off their watch, wrap it to the victim's wrist, check for a pulse, and then take off the watch and put it back on their wrist again to perform CPR. Ain't nobody got time for that.
  • What complications can occur? Vomiting is the most frequently encountered complication of CPR. The app doesn't elaborate beyond the compression stages. Perhaps adding a menu of quick tips can aid the user in case Plan A doesn't go smoothly.
