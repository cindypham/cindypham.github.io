CPR Guide -

Save a life, right at your hands

Client

Myself (passion project!)

Problem

According to the American Heart Association, nearly 383,000 out-of-hospital sudden cardiac arrests occur annually, and 88 percent of cardiac arrests occur at home. Unfortunately, 70% of Americans feel helpless to act during a cardiac emergency because they either do not know how to administer CPR or their training has significantly lapsed. When an emergency strikes, how can someone save a life if they don't have the exact compression pace ingrained in memory?

Despite smartwatches being questioned for their practicality, a wrist-bound gadget can prove to be invaluable during an emergency that requires both hands. CPR Guide is a smartwatch app I designed for the purpose of guiding users through the CPR process to encourage less hesitance and more confidence in taking action.

Process

Since time is critical during an emergency, the most important priority for user experience was using CPR Guide as quickly as possible. Therefore, the workflow of the app needed to be simple and easily understandable from a glance so that the user can efficiently attend to the patient.

I surveyed several people whose backgrounds ranged from highly experienced to little experienced and asked them to walk me through their thought process when dealing with a cardiac arrest victim. Their responses allowed me to visualize an ideal user scenario for my app that would benefit any user regardless of experience, as well as piece together a chronological order of steps in an emergency response that would make sense to everyone.

I drafted a workflow diagram for the app and brainstormed possible responses that the app could provide. I then eliminated any steps that would unnecessarily slow down the process. For example, my original draft had an extra step of calling 911 after the user verified that the patient had no pulse. I eliminated that option because it can be assumed that if the user is prepared to give CPR, the accident must have been dire enough for them to call 911 on their phone already, therefore making the app call emergency services again would be redundant. Providing tips for CPR was also unnecessary because the user would not have time to read through instructions. I wanted to focus strictly on small interactions with the watch and establish a simple, speedy process to achieve the main goal of the app, in which action must be taken immediately.

Solution

I focused strictly on establishing a smooth flow of the necessary responses needed to give CPR: checking the pulse, identifying the age group, and the actual compression performance.

Checking the pulse is the first step I added to the app because my survey results showed that emergency workers always checked for a pulse while inexperienced people would either forget to check for one before jumping into CPR or be uncertain about whether they can actually feel the pulse. Therefore, the CPR Guide app would have the option of measuring and reporting the heart rate of the patient if an inexperienced user was uncertain. Otherwise, the user can quickly proceed onto identifying the age group.

Reporting the age of the patient to the app is important to determine the pace of compressions that should be given. Once an age group is selected, the smartwatch will send pulses directly to the user's arm so that they know when to compress. This simple indicator is extremely effective in giving CPR without needing training or memorizing compression rates.
